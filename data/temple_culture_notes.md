# Temple Culture Explorer Knowledge Base

## Temple Architecture
Indian temple architecture often combines sacred geometry, sculpture, ritual movement, and local materials. The garbhagriha, or sanctum, houses the main deity and is usually the most sacred interior space. A mandapa is a pillared hall used for gathering, music, dance, prayer, and ceremonies. Many temples include a pradakshina path, allowing devotees to circumambulate the deity in a clockwise direction as an act of reverence.

## Dravidian Temple Style
Dravidian temples, common in South India, are known for towering gopurams, enclosed courtyards, pillared halls, temple tanks, and richly carved stone surfaces. The vimana rises above the sanctum, while the gopuram marks the gateway into the temple complex. Famous examples include Brihadeeswara Temple in Thanjavur, Meenakshi Amman Temple in Madurai, and the temples of Kanchipuram.

## Nagara Temple Style
Nagara temples, common in North India, often feature a curving shikhara above the sanctum, a raised platform, and a sequence of halls leading toward the deity. The vertical form of the shikhara symbolizes ascent and sacred presence. Examples include Kandariya Mahadeva Temple at Khajuraho, the Sun Temple at Konark, and several temples in Odisha, Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh, and Uttar Pradesh.

## Kalinga Temple Tradition
The Kalinga style, associated especially with Odisha, includes forms such as rekha deula for the sanctum tower, pidha deula for assembly spaces, and rich sculptural programs. Lingaraja Temple in Bhubaneswar, Jagannath Temple in Puri, and the Sun Temple at Konark show the ritual and artistic power of this tradition.

## Temple Rituals
Common temple rituals include darshan, aarti, abhishekam, alankaram, archana, and prasadam distribution. Darshan means seeing and being seen by the deity. Aarti uses flame as an offering and symbol of divine presence. Abhishekam is ritual bathing of the deity with substances such as water, milk, honey, or sandal paste, depending on the temple tradition.

## Festival Culture
Temple festivals often combine procession, music, dance, seasonal cycles, mythology, and community service. Rath yatras use chariots to bring the deity into public space. Brahmotsavam, Navaratri, Maha Shivaratri, Janmashtami, Vaikunta Ekadashi, and temple-specific annual festivals can draw large gatherings. Festival practices vary by region, sect, and temple history.

## Pilgrimage Etiquette
Visitors should dress modestly, remove footwear where required, follow photography rules, and observe local customs before entering sacred spaces. Many temples restrict entry into the sanctum or certain inner areas. It is respectful to keep phones silent, avoid touching sculptures or ritual objects without permission, and move with the flow of devotees during crowded darshan.

## Prasadam
Prasadam is food or another offering blessed in the temple and distributed to devotees. It represents grace, hospitality, and shared community. Famous examples include the laddu at Tirumala, mahaprasad at Puri Jagannath Temple, and pongal or puliyodarai in many South Indian temples. Rules for receiving and eating prasadam differ by temple.

## Temple Tanks
Temple tanks, also called pushkarini, kalyani, or kund depending on region, are water bodies connected to ritual purity, festivals, and traditional water management. Devotees may use tank water for symbolic purification where permitted. Temple tanks also support processions, float festivals, and the ecological life of temple towns.

## Dance and Music
Temples have historically supported classical arts, including Bharatanatyam, Odissi, Carnatic music, devotional singing, and percussion traditions. Dance, music, poetry, and sculpture often share mythological themes. Many temple festivals still include nadaswaram, mridangam, bhajan, kirtan, or regional performance traditions.

## Heritage Conservation
Temple conservation involves protecting stone, murals, woodwork, inscriptions, bronzes, water systems, and living ritual traditions. Good conservation respects both material heritage and active worship. Challenges include crowd pressure, pollution, inappropriate repairs, climate effects, and the need to document oral histories and local craft knowledge.

## Planning a Temple Visit
A thoughtful temple visit includes checking opening hours, festival schedules, dress rules, queue systems, and local transport. Morning and evening are often active ritual times. During major festivals, visitors should expect crowds, security checks, traffic restrictions, and longer waiting periods. Local guides can help explain history, iconography, and etiquette.
